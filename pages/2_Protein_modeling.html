Goal

The goal of this vignette is to provide some basic understanding of protein modeling as well as data wrangling and analysis in python.
Here I am interested in comparing how does mutation sites in protein receptor alters its ligand binding ability. The models used are generated by Alphafold.

Protein data files

When handling protein data. Depending on the preprocess, it's generally stored in the PDB file format. PDB files usually consists of the main information including the atom and the corresponding coordinates in a three dimensional space. And together all of these cooredinates is what makes up of a protein model in the 3D space.

There are a variety of method in obtaining protein structure (PDB file). Some include an actual true structure from cystalzation, and some are predictions via predictor models such as Alphafold.

Visualizing Protein structure

A simple way in visualizing the protein structure is simply plotting all the 3D coordinates. However, more often than not, doing so will result in a uninterpretable giant cloud due to the number of atoms there are.

Instead it's often more intuitive to simply plot the backbone of the amin acid not including the side chains. The backbone of the amino acid are simply made up of the atoms C, CA, CB, N, O.
Below is simply an interactive plot overlaying the two different protein structures generated by alphafold. Feel free to rotate around and explore the structure!

From the model it looks like the two models are quite similar which is a good start. However, olfactory receptor and protein folding in general is rather sensitive and selective. Any small movement or mutations deviating from the original form may lead to loss of function or failture to bind to ligands.

Amino acid mutations

In order to predict how well each amino acid is at interacting with a potential ligand, here we define a centroid in the binding cavity. Intuitively, we hypothesize that the more direct the amino acid side chain is pointing towards the center of the binding cavity the higher likelyhood that the amino acid is important for ligand binding interaction.

Here we are interested in seeing how does the mutation in the amino acid contributes to the overall structural differnce. Fortunately, we can infer the mutation quite easily by comparing the .fasta files.
From the 3D coordinates dataframe we have. We can specifically isolate for the mutated amino acids in the specific models.

From the plot, we can visualize while some mutations doesn't differ much in position but there are certainly some that resulted in a noticable difference in regarding to the amino acid position and directionality towards the center.

Quantifying difference

Now that we know the mutations do in fact changes the model's position, we next want to know whether how does these shifts changes the functionailty of the protein? If the amino acids still faces the center as well as before.
In order to quantify that, we can calculate and use the relative angle of the amino acid's side chain to the center as a proxy for directionality. And once we've calculated the angle for each model, we can take the differece and assign the value onto a ggplot for easier visualization.

The color scheme depicts the amount of degree the amino acids are each shifted away from the center relative to the corresponding amino acid. While the mutation sites are enlarged in bigger circles.

Conclusion

From this short vignette, we've briefly explored the PDB and how even simple 3D coordinate datasets can be visualized in a variety of ways. In the short analysis, we compared two protein models generated by using alphafold and deciphered how mutations may result in overall function of the protein.
